## Part 2 — Design Audit & Target Aesthetic

### Design Philosophy

Atticus should look like a tool that a senior patent attorney at a top IP firm would trust
on sight. Not a SaaS dashboard. Not an AI demo. A refined legal instrument.

**Reference touchstones:**
- **Harvey AI** — editorial gravitas, serif-forward typography, restrained palette,
  semantic design tokens, warm neutrals. Their principle: "precision in design reflects
  the precision our customers bring to their work." TWK Ghost (high-contrast serif)
  for display, ABC Diatype (clean sans) for UI. Near-blacks with warm amber accents.
  Atticus should share this level of intentionality but with a lighter, paper-forward
  working environment (Harvey's dark theme suits a marketing site; Atticus is a
  daily-use tool where users read long patent text).
- **Westlaw / LexisNexis** — professional legal research tools. Functional density
  done right, clear information hierarchy, no decorative elements.
- **The New York Times** — serif elegance, high readability at body-text sizes.
- **Stripe documentation** — clean structure, generous whitespace, clear hierarchy
  (for layout and spacing principles, not color).
- **Old money law library** — the overall feeling. Green leather desk accessories,
  mahogany, aged paper, brass fixtures, a barrister's reading lamp.

**What the design should signal:**
- Edited, verified, authoritative — not generated by AI
- Wealthy, clean, smooth — institutional confidence
- Educated and precise — patent prosecution demands exactness
- Established and trustworthy — even though it's new software

---

### Color Palette — "Patent Library"

The palette draws from the visual language of institutional patent law: the green
of aged bronze on a federal building, the ivory of archival paper, the warmth of
mahogany and brass. It avoids generic SaaS blue (every legal tool uses it),
bright crimson (too close to Harvard), and any high-saturation or neon colors.

The green-forward palette is deliberately uncommon in legal tech. Most competitors
default to navy. Forest green signals growth and innovation (fitting for patents),
wealth and assets (fitting for IP valuation), and balance and precision (fitting
for prosecution arguments) — while remaining distinctly institutional.

```
Backgrounds
  bgPrimary:     #FAF8F4    — warm ivory; aged paper, not screen-white
  bgSecondary:   #F0ECE4    — parchment; subtle section backgrounds
  bgWhite:       #FFFFFF    — pure white for cards/inputs that need to "lift" off parchment

Sidebar & primary dark
  sidebar:       #1C2420    — near-black with green undertone (not pure black)

Text
  textPrimary:   #1C2420    — same as sidebar; cohesive dark
  textSecondary: #5A5F57    — warm gray-green; readable but recessive

Brand accent — deep forest green
  accent:        #2B5940    — the primary brand color: deep muted forest green
  accentHover:   #1E3F2D    — one shade darker for hover/pressed states
  accentSubtle:  #E8F0EB    — very light green tint for selected states, highlights

Verification status
  verified:      #2B5940    — same as brand accent (verified = trusted = Atticus)
  review:        #8C6B2E    — warm antiqued gold/amber (attention, not alarm)
  unverified:    #7D3040    — deep muted burgundy (serious, not screaming)
                              NOTE: this is NOT Harvard crimson (#A51C30);
                              it's darker, cooler, and more restrained

Borders & dividers
  borderc:       #D4CFC6    — warm stone; hairline borders, never prominent

Supplementary (use sparingly)
  gold:          #8B7355    — antique brass; used for subtle metallic accents,
                              important labels, or premium indicators
```

**Why this palette over alternatives:**
- Forest green (#2B5940) sits between USPTO's institutional blue and the
  warm amber of Harvey AI — distinctly its own identity
- The warm ivory backgrounds (#FAF8F4) are warmer than typical SaaS white
  (#FAFAFA) and cooler than actual parchment — comfortable for long reading
- The sidebar green-black (#1C2420) picks up the green undertone, making the
  dark chrome feel connected to the accent rather than arbitrarily dark
- Using the brand green for "verified" status creates a subliminal association:
  the brand color = trust. Verified items feel native; unverified items feel
  foreign (they're in a different color family entirely)
- The burgundy for unverified (#7D3040) is deliberately muted — it reads as
  "this needs attention" not "this is an error." Patent prosecution is nuanced;
  an unverified claim isn't necessarily wrong, just unconfirmed

### Typography

The type system should feel like a well-typeset legal journal. Serif-forward
for authority, with sans-serif for functional UI elements.

Current setup: Merriweather (headings) + Inter (body) + JetBrains Mono (code).
Files: `frontend/tailwind.config.js`, `frontend/src/index.css`.

**Recommended type stack:**

```
Headings / Display     Libre Baskerville (700)
                       — based on ATF Baskerville, the same tradition as the
                         typefaces used in court filings and legal treatises
                         for over a century. Designed specifically for screen
                         readability. More refined and lighter than Merriweather,
                         which reads slightly heavy/round for this aesthetic.
                       — Fallback: Georgia, "Times New Roman", serif

Body text              Source Serif 4 (400, 600)
                       — Adobe's open-source transitional serif. Variable font
                         with optical sizing: automatically adjusts letterforms
                         for small text (footnotes, fine print) vs. display sizes.
                         Modern precision rooted in classical serif tradition.
                         Pairs beautifully with Libre Baskerville headings.
                       — For UI chrome (navigation labels, button text, form
                         labels): Inter remains the right choice — clean, neutral,
                         doesn't compete with the serif hierarchy.
                       — Fallback: Georgia, "Palatino Linotype", serif

Monospace              JetBrains Mono (400, 500)
                       — for patent numbers, application numbers, claim text,
                         code references. No change needed.
                       — Fallback: "Courier New", monospace
```

**Alternative heading option:** Cormorant Garamond (600, 700) — even more
editorial and "law review" than Libre Baskerville. Higher contrast, more
presence. Best for firms that want to project a sense of long-established
tradition. Trade-off: not optimized for small sizes, so strictly headings only.

**Key decision: serif or sans-serif body?**
- Serif body (Source Serif 4) = full editorial/institutional feel. Matches the
  "law review" aesthetic perfectly. Better for the long-form patent text and
  argument drafts that dominate the response drafter view.
- Sans-serif body (Inter) = more modern/functional. Better for UI-dense views
  like the claim mapping table and settings page.
- **Recommended hybrid:** Source Serif 4 for all paragraph text (analysis
  descriptions, drafted arguments, MPEP excerpts). Inter for UI elements
  (navigation, buttons, labels, table headers, badges). This mirrors how
  Harvey AI separates TWK Ghost (editorial) from ABC Diatype (functional).

**Google Fonts import:**
```css
@import url("https://fonts.googleapis.com/css2?family=Libre+Baskerville:wght@400;700&family=Source+Serif+4:opsz,wght@8..60,400;8..60,600&family=Inter:wght@400;500;600&family=JetBrains+Mono:wght@400;500&display=swap");
```

### Layout & Component Principles

**General spacing:**
- Generous whitespace everywhere. The current `max-w-2xl` / `max-w-4xl`
  containers with `px-6 py-12` are reasonable. Err toward more breathing room.
- Line height for body text: 1.7–1.8 (legal text needs air).
- Paragraph spacing: `mb-6` minimum for drafted text.

**Cards & sections (RejectionCard, etc.):**
- Should feel like sections of a well-typeset document, not dashboard widgets.
- Border radius: `rounded` (4px) maximum. Prefer `rounded-sm` (2px) or none.
- No drop shadows. Separation comes from subtle borders or background shifts.
- Thin borders (`border border-borderc`) or just top/bottom divider lines.
- Consider no border at all — just a subtle background shift (`bg-bgSecondary`
  on the card vs. `bg-bgPrimary` on the page).

**Buttons:**
- Primary: solid `bg-accent text-white` — firm but not loud. `rounded-sm`.
- Secondary: `border border-borderc` — borderless or very lightly bordered.
- No `rounded-full` pill buttons anywhere. `rounded-sm` or `rounded`.
- No gradient backgrounds on buttons.
- Button text in Inter (sans-serif), not serif.

**Verification badges (VerificationBadge.jsx):**
- Small, quiet indicators — think footnote markers, not traffic lights.
- A small colored dot (6px) next to a text label, or a subtle text-only label.
- Not large colored badges or pill tags.
- Colors should be the muted verification palette (verified green, review
  amber, unverified burgundy) — never high-saturation.

**Source Viewer modal:**
- Should feel like opening a reference document — clean, readable, with clear
  provenance (patent number, column/line reference as a header).
- Not a popup dialog with an overlay shadow. A slide-in panel or a clean modal
  with minimal chrome.
- Source text displayed in Source Serif 4 (it's a document, treat it like one).

**Sidebar:**
- Dark sidebar with "ATTICUS" in letter-spaced caps is correct direction.
- The letter-spacing and font should read as "refined institution" not
  "tech startup." Evaluate whether the current `tracking-[0.2em]` is
  right — compare with how Harvey AI treats its wordmark.
- Recent analyses list: clean and scannable. Minimal visual clutter.
  The rejection-basis pills (`§103`) are good but should be very subtle —
  near-transparent background, not bright.

**Page headers:**
- The `Application 19531961 — non-final rejection` header is well-structured.
- Consider a thin horizontal rule below it (like a legal document header)
  rather than a full `border-b`.

### What to Avoid

These are anti-patterns that would undermine the institutional feel:

- Border radius > 4px (reads as consumer app)
- Gradient backgrounds (reads as marketing page)
- Animated transitions beyond subtle fades (reads as demo)
- High-contrast colored badges or tags (reads as project management tool)
- Any element that looks like it was generated by a Tailwind UI template
- Rounded-full pill buttons or tags
- Blue accent (#1E40AF or similar) — this is generic SaaS; the green is the brand
- Drop shadows on cards
- Playful or geometric sans-serif fonts
- Emoji in the UI (except possibly in empty states, sparingly)
- "AI" branding or sparkle icons — this tool should look like a legal instrument,
  not an AI product

---

### Priority Design Changes (ranked by impact)

If only 5 changes can be made, these most shift the feel from "AI SaaS tool"
to "trusted legal instrument":

1. **Swap accent from blue to forest green (#2B5940)** — instantly
   differentiates from every other legal tech tool and establishes the
   institutional identity.

2. **Replace Merriweather with Libre Baskerville for headings** — lighter,
   more refined, closer to the typefaces actually used in legal documents.
   Immediately shifts the feel from "blog" to "law review."

3. **Warm the backgrounds from #FAFAF9 to #FAF8F4** — subtle but powerful.
   The warmer ivory reads as paper, not screen. Combined with serif headings,
   this creates the "printed document" feeling.

4. **Add Source Serif 4 for body text in document-like areas** — the analysis
   overview, drafted arguments, and MPEP excerpts should use a serif body.
   This is the single change that most makes the text feel "edited and
   verified" rather than "generated."

5. **Reduce border-radius to 2px and remove any shadows** — the cards and
   buttons should feel like sections of a typeset document. Rounded corners
   and shadows are hallmarks of the consumer-app aesthetic.
